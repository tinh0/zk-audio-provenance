Wildlife Trust records rare forest chorus at dawn

An unbroken three-minute recording from the Olympic Peninsula captures the return of a breeding pair of marbled murrelets — and the acoustic signature researchers have been chasing for a decade.

By Rowan Stiles · Staff Correspondent · April 15, 2026

Just after 5:14 a.m. on Tuesday, ecologists from the Olympic Wildlife Trust pressed record. Three minutes and twelve seconds later they had what they came for: the layered, high-frequency keening of a nesting pair of marbled murrelets returning to a single Sitka spruce canopy — a sound unheard on the peninsula since 2015.

Listen · Dawn chorus recording

The trust's acoustic monitoring program, now in its ninth year, uses attested-capture microphones developed in partnership with the University of Washington. Every recording is signed at the hardware level, and subsequent edits — trimming, volume normalization, noise reduction — carry cryptographic receipts so that scientific claims downstream can be independently verified.

"The question we always got from peer reviewers," said trust director Dr. Nalia Chen, "was whether our field audio had been selectively cleaned up. With the new provenance chain we can publish the chorus exactly as it was captured, edits and all, and anyone with a browser can verify the math."

The recording has been submitted to the Journal of Applied Ecology with its full provenance manifest intact.
